About Information

Sarawak, Malaysia's largest state, captivates with its mystical charm and lush landscapes on the southwest ridge of Borneo. The state is renowned for its expansive jungles, massive natural formations, and the world's largest cave chambers, alongside its unique flora and fauna. Sarawak's rich natural heritage is highlighted by its UNESCO World Heritage Site and numerous protected national parks, while its abundance of hornbills has earned it the title 'Land of the Hornbills.'

Kuching, the vibrant capital, offers a glimpse into Sarawak's intriguing history, shaped by its diverse ethnic groups and the legacy of the Brooke dynasty. Whether you're exploring its extraordinary natural wonders or delving into its cultural past, Sarawak promises a captivating experience.

Sematan Beach

Located about 120 kilometres (75 miles) from Kuching, Sarawak, Sematan Beach is a beloved getaway for locals, situated near the border with Kalimantan. Originally a fishing village, it has evolved into an exclusive resort destination. The beach, known for its pristine and litter-free condition, extends along the South China Sea and is frequently ranked among the best beaches in Sarawak.

Hawaii Beach

Located in Kampong Bakam, just 15 minutes from Miri City Centre, Hawaii Beach in Sarawak lives up to its name with its white sands and curved palm trees. The beach features a footpath with seating areas and barbecue spots, perfect for a relaxing day out. Visitors can reach Hawaii Beach by taxi from Miri or by taking the number 13 bus.

Damai Beach

Nestled within the Damai Beach Resort, Damai Beach is renowned as the most beautiful beach in Kuching. Its pristine orange sand curves along the edge of dense Borneo rainforests, providing an ultra-luxurious experience. The beach is impeccably maintained for the resort's guests, with huts and sun beds available. Situated next to Mount Santubong, Damai Beach is a favorite spot for Kuching residents and is just under an hour's drive from the city center.

Borneo Cultures Museum

Opened in March 2022, the Borneo Cultures Museum is the second largest museum in Southeast Asia, featuring over 1,000 artifacts that highlight the rich cultural heritage of Borneo and Sarawak's indigenous peoples. Spanning five levels, each dedicated to unique themes and exhibitions, the museum offers an essential experience for culture enthusiasts.

Sibu Central Market

The Sibu Central Market, Malaysia's largest indoor market, houses thousands of stores under one roof. Merging the former wet market and Lembangan market into a modern, hygienic space, it features a diverse array of stalls selling vegetables, fruits, meats, general merchandise, and a variety of food and drinks.

Kuching Old Court House

The Kuching Old Court House, now the Sarawak Tourism Complex, once served as Sarawak's judicial center. Visitors can admire its beautiful colonial architecture, enjoy a coffee at one of the on-site cafes, and explore the various museums housed within the complex.

Carpenter Street

A visit to Carpenter Street in Kuching feels like a step back in time. The street is lined with kopitiams, antiques, and traditional handicrafts, each shop offering a glimpse into Sarawak's history. Carpenter Street connects to Ewe Hai Street, one of the city's oldest roads, where the rich culture and deep history come alive.

Bako National Park

Bako National Park, the oldest in Sarawak, is a must-visit destination just 37 kilometers from Kuching city. Officially established in 1957, this 27-square-kilometer park, while small, boasts a remarkable diversity of flora and fauna, including 600 flower species and 267 wildlife species like proboscis monkeys and silver leaf monkeys. Its white sandy beaches, such as Telok Pandan Kecil, are framed by stunning rock formations and offer breathtaking views.

The park features 18 color-coded walking trails, catering to all levels of hikers and adventurers. Notable highlights include unique geological formations like sea stacks and arches, sculpted by millennia of erosion. With opportunities for both day hikes and overnight camping, Bako National Park provides a rich exploration of Borneo’s rainforest. The park is open daily from 8:00 am to 5:00 pm, including public holidays.

Niah National Park

Niah National Park in Miri, Sarawak, is a significant archaeological site renowned for its ancient human remains and prehistoric relics. Established as a national park in 1974, it covers 3,138 hectares of forest and limestone karst areas. The park's West Mouth of the Great Cave, where a modern human skull estimated to be around 40,000 years old was discovered in 1958, offers a fascinating glimpse into Southeast Asia's early human history. The park's extensive cave system, including the Traders’ Cave and the Great Cave, reveals the remnants of ancient human activity and shelters once used by birds’ nest collectors.

The Great Cave features an impressive entrance at 60 metres high and 250 metres wide, leading to the spectacular Padang Chamber, where sunlight creates dramatic effects on the rock formations. The Moon Cave, accessed via a plank walkway, leads to the Painted Cave, adorned with ancient paintings of forest animals and believed to be a burial site with remnants of boat-shaped coffins. Niah National Park is a captivating destination for both nature lovers and history enthusiasts, offering a unique blend of natural beauty and archaeological significance.

Gunung Mulu National Park

Gunung Mulu National Park, spanning 528.64 square kilometres in Miri, Sarawak, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site celebrated for its remarkable geodiversity and biodiversity. Renowned for its extensive cave systems, including the Sarawak Chamber—the world's largest natural underground chamber—the park also features some of the most studied tropical karst formations globally. Its limestone pinnacles, such as those on Mount Api, Mount Benarat, and the majestic Mount Mulu, along with the Garden of Eden's striking karst collapse, are truly awe-inspiring.

In addition to its stunning caves, the park offers adventurous treks like the Pinnacles Summit Trek, Headhunter’s Trails, and Gunung Mulu Summit Trek. Visitors can also enjoy the Mulu Canopy Walk, a 480-metre walkway suspended 25 metres above the forest floor, making it one of the longest tree-based canopy walks in the world.

Bung Jagoi

Nestled about 20 kilometers from Kuching in Bau, Bung Jagoi is a historic village steeped in Dayak Bidayuh heritage. Established over 175 years ago, it sits atop a hill 280 meters above sea level, surrounded by pristine forest. This ancestral home of the Bidayuh people, who migrated from Mount Sungkung in Indonesia, is renowned for its cultural significance and historical relics, including the round-shaped baruk (skull house), which once held trophies from ancient warriors and now serves as a cultural preservation site.

Visitors to Bung Jagoi can experience the traditional Bidayuh lifestyle by exploring the remnants of old houses and participating in cultural rituals. The village is accessible via a hike from Kampung Duyoh, where visitors can register and prepare for the journey. Activities include staying at homestays, visiting local craft galleries, and trekking to Bung Tesen for breathtaking sunset views. Additionally, eco-tourism options like night walks, waterfall treks, and traditional meals provide a rich, immersive experience into the Bidayuh culture and the natural beauty of the area.

Sarawak Cultural Village

Sarawak, Malaysia's largest state, is home to a diverse population of 2.8 million people, comprising 26 ethnic groups. Notable among these are the Iban and Bidayuh Dayaks, as well as the Orang Ulu, Melanau, Murut, and several others. This rich ethnic tapestry makes Sarawak a captivating destination for cultural exploration.

Established in 1991, the Sarawak Cultural Village offers an immersive experience into the state's diverse cultures. Situated at the foot of Mount Santubong, this award-winning living museum spans 17 acres and features replicas of traditional houses representing the Iban, Bidayuh, Penan, and other groups. Visitors can explore these cultural exhibits, participate in traditional crafts, and enjoy performances by an award-winning dance troupe, gaining deep insights into Sarawak's heritage.

Siniawan Food Festival

Located about 20 km from Kuching in the Bau district, Siniawan is a quaint town that retains the charm of old Sarawak with its row of wooden shopfronts. By day, it's a serene place, but every other weekend, its central street transforms into a vibrant market with food stalls offering a variety of local delights.

Among the must-try dishes are pitcher plants stuffed with steamed glutinous rice, a traditional Bidayuh treat, and kompiah, mini burger-like buns filled with meat and other fillings. Halal options are also available from Muslim traders, allowing visitors to enjoy their meals in an open-air setting amidst the town's historic architecture.

Tua Pek Kong Chinese Temple

Tua Pek Kong Chinese Temple, also known as Vihara Budhi Bhakti, has been a prominent Buddhist site since its inauguration in 1986. Popular with both local and international visitors, the temple offers free entry.

Kuching Mosque

The Kuching Mosque, also known as the Old State Mosque, is a prominent landmark in Kuching city. Featuring simple wooden architecture, it attracts many visitors and remains bustling throughout the day. Entry to the mosque is free.
